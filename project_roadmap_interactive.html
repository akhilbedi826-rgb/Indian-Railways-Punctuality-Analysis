Descriptive stats
Mean, median, mode, variance, std, skewness (+ve skew expected), kurtosis. Present as a formatted DataFrame — one table per feature.
scipy.statspandas
Test 2 ✦
Kruskal-Wallis test
H₀: delay is the same across regions. Use Kruskal-Wallis (not ANOVA) because delay data is not normally distributed. Write the interpretation in plain English.
scipy.stats
Test 3 ✦
Chi-square test
H₀: train type and delay category are independent. Chi-square on a contingency table. Shows if Rajdhani and Passenger actually have statistically different delay patterns.
scipy.stats.chi2
Optional
Linear regression
Predict Arrival_Delay_Min from Distance_KM, Is_Peak_Hour, Is_Weekend, Season. Report R², MAE. One-level above college without being ML-expert level.
sklearnstatsmodels
How to write up hypothesis tests: State H₀ and H₁ clearly. Show the test statistic and p-value. Then write a plain-English conclusion like "With p < 0.001, we reject the null hypothesis — delay patterns differ significantly across regions, suggesting structural differences in railway operations."

Insights
Business insights — where marks are earned
Don't just state what you see — explain what it means operationally
Weak insight
"North has high delay"
Just states a fact. No depth. No action. Anyone could read this off a bar chart.
Strong insight ✦
"Northern zone averages 31 min delay"
Northern zone averages 31 min delay — 38% above the national average of 22 min — likely driven by high network congestion between Delhi and Kanpur, which handle 45% of North zone traffic.
Weak insight
"Monsoon has more delays"
Obvious. Everyone knows rain causes delays. You need the magnitude and implication.
Strong insight ✦
"Monsoon delays 45% higher"
Monsoon season (June–September) shows 45% higher average delay than winter — with Northeast Frontier Railway most impacted at 2.1× winter levels — suggesting flood-prone track sections need infrastructure priority.

5 strong insights to include in your report

1. Vande Bharat reliability: VB trains average 5.5 min delay vs 44.5 min for Passenger trains — an 8× difference — demonstrating that modern rolling stock significantly reduces punctuality variance.

2. Route accumulation effect: Delay increases 12% per intermediate station — trains accumulate delays along routes, meaning last-stop stations suffer most regardless of origin performance.

3. Weekend paradox: Weekends show 10% higher delays despite lower freight — passenger crowding at major junctions (HWH, NDLS, MAS) likely causes longer dwell times.

4. Peak hour compounding: 7–10 AM and 5–8 PM arrivals show 15% longer delays — converging train schedules at hub stations create bottlenecks that cascade through the day.

5. Zone paradox: Southern Railway has the most trains but near-average delays — indicating that network volume alone doesn't predict punctuality; infrastructure investment matters more.
